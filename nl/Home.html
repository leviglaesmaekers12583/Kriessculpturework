About Kries

Kries was born in Norwich. She completed her training as a registered nurse in Norwich in 1980. Afterwards she started teaching herself how to be a sculptor. Nowadays she works as an accountant but she also likes to make scultpures in her free time. She also sells these sculptures at local fairs in the Netherlands where she resides now.

How i do it . . .

Manner of working

Her sculptures are abstract represantations of things she ‘sees’ in the stone. When Kries first gets a stone she tries to get a feel of what the object inside of it ‘is’ and then tries to extract that sculpture from the raw stone by only removing the minimal amount of material. The interplay of materials and shapes are the things that interest Kries the most. Texture, colour and form all combine into one in her artworks. The first artwork she made was “Nelly the hippo” which she made at a workshop. She's been hooked on sculpting ever since.

Inspiration

Kries's images also reflect her environment and where she lives. She moved to Heerlen several years ago and her scultpures reflect herself as much as the environment around her.

Serpentine

Serpentine is a general term for several subspecies. They each have a unique appearance and require different forces for processing. Most types come to the Netherlands from Zimbabwe.
Serpentine comes in different colors there are serpentines that also remain brown in depth, and dark brown serpentine.

Springstone

Jumping stones are also often counted among the serpentine types. Editing Springstone takes the most energy. The cut pieces fly off and become quite sharp. Springstone is a rock with weight, both literally and figuratively. Well polished and waxed, Featherstone has a beautiful deep black color.

Ruwinka

Ruwinka is also from Zimbabwe. Ruwinka is a joy to use, but it can be a bit layered, so you need to make sure everything is going your way. Ruwinka is a type with a dark hardness and a highly transparent brown-blue, brown-grey or brown-green color. These colors come into their own very well when rubbing stones.

Soapstone

Soapstone can be found on every continent and in almost every color imaginable. Soapstone is generally considered a poor choice for garden sculpture. But I think you can keep a wide variety of species out if you wax the statues well and don't have any holes or grooves for water to collect in during the winter. Alabaster

Most people only know white alabaster. transparent or opaque. However, there are many types of alabaster, all possible colors and more or less transparent. The outer layer may contain many impurities, gray inclusions. White alabaster is often sawn into flat discs and sold as is. Alabaster is a fairly soft glassy stone.
